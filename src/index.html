Introduction

The proboscis monkey (Nasalis larvatus) or long nosed monkey is a rainforest primate found only in Borneo. Because of its distinctive appearance and its unique adaptations, it is a species of considerable scientific interest, especially for its physical and behavioral adaptations to a particular and often threatened habitat.

Habitat and Distribution

Borneo is an island shared by Indonesia, Malaysia and Brunei and the proboscis monkey is endemic to the island. They are found mainly in lowland rainforest, swamp and mangrove forest close to river and coastal areas. It is important to be so close to water as proboscis monkeys are one of the best swimmers among primates, using their partially webbed feet to swim rivers and escape predators. But they depend on particular habitats, and habitat loss from deforestation and land conversion for agriculture makes them vulnerable.

Diet

The proboscis monkey is largely folivorous, but with some seasonal dietary shifts to include fruits, seeds and occasionally flowers, in their diet. Because their digestive systems are specialized to remove nutrients from cellulose in fibrous vegetation that other animals might find more trouble digesting, they donâ€™t need to be frightened of being prey. Young leaves are preferred, since they are easier to digest. When available, proboscis monkeys will eat fruit, but too much fruit can throw them out of balance. This selectivity in food makes them more dependent on the kinds of vegetation they must have, and thus the native habitats.

Behaviour and Social Structure

Proboscis monkeys are socially grouped, or haremed, usually comprised of one dominant male and several females and their offspring. Security and reproductive success of dominant male are provided by this structure. All members of the group help protect and nurture infants, although females are the only ones known to be the primary caregivers of their young. Bachelors groups of young males, in which males remain until they are able to form harems, are also common. This group dynamic is vital to their survival: if they didn't have it, each wildebeest would be much more likely to be bitten, killed and fed to a crocodile or a bird of prey.

Reproduction and Life Cycle

Female proboscis monkeys become sexually mature at about five years of age, and males not until about seven years of age. The species has a gestation period of approximately 166 days, and has a litter size of one offspring and mothers produce only one offspring at a time. Blue faces and dark fur are distinctive of infants, but change as they mature. It's important for maternal care during the early stages: young monkeys tend to stick with their mother, they don't want to get far away from her, and they gradually pull away to become a part of a group as they get bigger.

Conservation Status

Proboscis monkeys are classified as Endangered on the IUCN Red List for a number of reasons, the most important of which is their continued loss of habitat due to hunting and human encroachment. Their available habitats have been greatly reduced by deforestation, especially for palm oil plantations. Their populations have been affected too: by hunting for food and the illegal pet trade. These primates need to be conserved, and some success has been achieved in creating reserves and protected areas on Borneo.
